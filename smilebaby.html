Baby Tooth Timeline

When do baby teeth come in?

Most babies get all 20 primary teeth by age 3. Here's what to expect and when — though every baby is different and timing can vary by several months.

🦷
Lower Central Incisors
6–10 months
Usually the very first teeth to appear. Look for drooling and chewing on everything.
🦷
Upper Central Incisors
8–12 months
Often arrive close behind the lower ones. Baby may be more fussy during this period.
🦷
Upper Lateral Incisors
9–13 months
The teeth on either side of the upper centrals. Teething discomfort may increase.
🦷
Lower Lateral Incisors
10–16 months
Completes the front four on the bottom row. Brushing twice daily should begin now.
🦷
First Molars
13–19 months
Back teeth are larger and can cause more discomfort. A chilled teething ring may help.
🦷
Canines (Cuspids)
16–23 months
The pointed teeth between incisors and molars. Teething can be more intense at this stage.
🦷
Second Molars
23–33 months
The last teeth to arrive — and often the most uncomfortable. Most babies are close to age 2–3 by now.
🌟
Full Set Complete!
By age 3
20 baby teeth total — 10 on top, 10 on bottom. First dental visit should have happened by now.
Age-by-Age Care Guide

What to do at every stage

Oral care needs change fast in the first three years. Here's exactly what to do and when.

Birth to 6 Months

Before first tooth
Wipe gums with a damp soft cloth after each feeding
Never put baby to sleep with a bottle — formula and breast milk both pool and cause decay
Avoid sharing spoons or pacifiers — bacteria in your mouth can transfer to baby's
Watch for signs of first tooth: drooling, chewing, fussiness

6 to 12 Months

First teeth arriving
Brush as soon as first tooth appears — use a soft infant toothbrush
Use a tiny smear of fluoride toothpaste (rice grain sized) twice daily
Schedule baby's first dental visit by their first birthday or 6 months after first tooth
Offer water in a sippy cup — start transitioning away from bottle
No juice before 12 months — even 100% juice is high in sugar

12 Months to Age 3

Toddler years
Increase to pea-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste at age 3
Brush twice daily — morning and before bed
Limit sugary snacks and juice to mealtimes only
Regular dental visits every 6 months
Start teaching toddler to spit — not swallow — toothpaste
Discourage thumb-sucking and pacifier use after age 2
Teething Guide

What actually helps — and what to avoid

The teething product market is full of things that don't work and some that are genuinely dangerous. Here's what the evidence actually says.

✅ Safe & Effective
🧊

Chilled (not frozen) teething rings

A refrigerated (not frozen) silicone teething ring can soothe gum inflammation. Frozen rings are too hard and can hurt baby's gums.

✅ Safe & Effective
🖐️

Clean finger gum massage

Gently rubbing baby's gums with a clean finger provides counter-pressure that many babies find soothing. Simple and effective.

✅ Safe & Effective
🧸

Safe teething toys

Look for one-piece silicone or rubber toys with no small parts. Avoid liquid-filled rings — they can break and leak.

⚠️ Avoid
💊

Benzocaine teething gels

The FDA warns against benzocaine products for children under 2. They can cause a serious blood condition called methemoglobinemia.

⚠️ Avoid
📿

Amber teething necklaces

These are a strangulation and choking hazard with no proven benefit. The AAP and FDA advise against them entirely.

⚠️ Avoid
🌿

Homeopathic teething tablets

Some homeopathic products have been recalled for containing belladonna. Stick to evidence-based approaches only.

First Dentist Visit

When and what to expect

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends baby's first dental visit by their first birthday — or within 6 months of the first tooth appearing. Here's how to prepare.

01

When to go

By age 1, or within 6 months of the first tooth. Don't wait until there's a problem — early visits build familiarity and trust.

02

What happens

The dentist will examine teeth, gums, and jaw development. They'll check for early decay, talk about diet and habits, and demonstrate proper brushing.

03

How to prepare

Read books about dentist visits beforehand. Schedule in the morning when baby is rested. Bring a familiar toy or comfort item. Stay calm — babies pick up on parent anxiety.

04

After the visit

Return every 6 months. Ask about fluoride supplements if your water isn't fluoridated. Follow up on any concerns the dentist raised right away.

💡 Questions to Ask at the First Visit
Is my baby's tooth development on track?
Does my water have enough fluoride?
How do I clean teeth at this age properly?
Are there any habits I should change now?
What foods should I limit or avoid?
When should pacifier or thumb use stop?
