Dental Readiness Classification

Your dental class determines your deployability

The US military classifies every service member's dental health on a 1–4 scale. Classes 3 and 4 mean you cannot deploy. Understanding your classification — and how to improve it — is mission-critical.

Class 1 — Deployable

No dental problems

No current dental issues and no anticipated problems within 12 months. Fully deployable. This is where every service member should be. Achieved through consistent oral hygiene and 6-month checkups.

Class 2 — Deployable

Needs treatment — stable

Has dental conditions that need treatment but are not expected to cause a dental emergency within 12 months. Deployable but should seek treatment soon. Most soldiers fall here at some point.

Class 3 — Non-Deployable

Likely emergency within 12 months

Has conditions likely to cause a dental emergency within 12 months — infections, severe decay, broken teeth. Cannot deploy until treated. This is one of the most common reasons for deployment delays.

Class 4 — Needs exam

No current exam on file

No dental exam within the required period. Cannot be classified or deployed until examined. Annual exams are mandatory — missing them automatically triggers non-deployable status.

The VA Dental Gap

Most veterans don't qualify
for VA dental care

Despite serving their country, the majority of veterans are not eligible for VA dental benefits. The result is a silent epidemic of untreated oral disease in the veteran population.

The Coverage Gap

VA dental benefits are only available to veterans with service-connected dental conditions, former POWs, veterans rated 100% disabled, or those in specific VA programs. Most veterans — even those with decades of service — do not qualify.

The Health Consequence

Veterans have significantly higher rates of tooth loss, gum disease, and oral cancer than the general population. Untreated oral disease contributes to heart disease, diabetes complications, and depression — conditions already prevalent in veterans.

The Mental Health Link

Tooth loss and poor oral health are independently associated with depression, social withdrawal, and reduced quality of life. For veterans already managing PTSD and mental health challenges, untreated dental problems compound the burden significantly.

Free Alternatives That Exist

Dental schools offer high-quality, low-cost care. Federally Qualified Health Centers provide sliding-scale dental services. The VA's Dental Insurance Program (VADIP) offers subsidized private dental insurance. Most veterans don't know these options exist.

Who qualifies for VA dental?

VA dental eligibility is limited but important to understand. You may qualify if:

✓You have a service-connected dental disability or condition
✓You are a former prisoner of war (POW)
✓You have a VA combined disability rating of 100%
✓You are enrolled in a VA vocational rehabilitation program
✓You are homeless and enrolled in certain VA programs
💡 Don't qualify? Ask your VA about VADIP — the VA Dental Insurance Program — which offers subsidized private dental insurance for veterans who don't qualify for free care.
Mental Health & Oral Health

PTSD destroys teeth.
Nobody talks about it.

The connection between combat trauma, PTSD, and oral health is severe — and almost completely ignored in both mental health and dental care settings.

😬

Bruxism (Teeth Grinding)

PTSD causes hypervigilance and jaw clenching — especially during sleep. Veterans grind their teeth at rates far higher than the general population, leading to cracked teeth, worn enamel, TMJ disorders, and chronic facial pain. Most are never told the connection.

💊

Medication-Induced Dry Mouth

Antidepressants, antianxiety medications, and sleep aids — all commonly prescribed to veterans — cause severe dry mouth (xerostomia). Dry mouth dramatically increases cavity risk and gum infection. Veterans on long-term psychiatric medications need more frequent dental care.

😰

Dental Anxiety in Veterans

The sounds, smells, and loss of control in a dental chair can trigger trauma responses in combat veterans. Dental anxiety in veterans is a clinical reality — and a major reason many avoid care until problems become emergencies. Trauma-informed dental care matters.

Field Triage Guide

Managing dental emergencies
when there's no dentist

For deployed service members and combat medics — a clear guide to what's urgent, what can wait, and what to do in the field when dental care is not available.

🔴 Urgent — Seek Care ASAP

Dental Abscess

Swelling of the face or jaw, fever, severe throbbing pain, difficulty swallowing or breathing. This is life-threatening if untreated — bacteria can spread to the airway and brain. Evacuate for dental care immediately. Antibiotics (amoxicillin or clindamycin if penicillin-allergic) should be initiated.

🔴 Urgent — Seek Care ASAP

Knocked-Out Tooth (Avulsion)

Hold tooth by the crown — never the root. Rinse gently with clean water. Reinsert into socket immediately if possible, or store in milk/saliva. Get to a dentist within 30–60 minutes — every minute outside the socket reduces survival chance. Do not let it dry out.

🟡 Monitor — Treat & Watch

Cracked or Chipped Tooth

If no pain: cover with dental wax or sugar-free gum as temporary protection. Avoid chewing on that side. If pain is present or nerve is exposed (sharp sustained pain): treat as urgent. Dental cement from a field kit can temporarily seal exposed dentine.

🟡 Monitor — Treat & Watch

Lost Filling or Crown

If available, use temporary dental cement (OTC or field kit) to reseat the crown or fill the cavity temporarily. Avoid sugar and extreme temperatures. Pain indicates nerve exposure — escalate to urgent. Most lost fillings can wait 24–72 hours if no pain.

🟢 Preventable — Field Hygiene

Gum Infection (Gingivitis/Periodontitis)

Bleeding gums, swelling, bad breath. Brush twice daily even in the field — a dry brush is better than no brush. Salt water rinses reduce bacterial load. Chlorhexidine rinse if available. Untreated gum infections become abscesses — prevent before they escalate.

🟢 Preventable — Field Hygiene

Toothache (Non-Abscess)

Ibuprofen (400–600mg) and acetaminophen together provide better pain control than either alone. Clove oil on a cotton ball applied to the tooth provides temporary relief. Identify the cause — decay, cracking, or gum recession. Do not ignore a persistent toothache: it will become an abscess.

⚠️ Medical Disclaimer

This field guide is for educational and general awareness purposes only. It does not replace formal combat casualty care training (TC 4-02.1) or the judgment of trained medical personnel. Always follow your unit's MEDEVAC and sick call protocols. When in doubt, evacuate.
